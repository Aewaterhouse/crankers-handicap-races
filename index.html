Race 04 Report

Angus Barton has won a bike race. Read that again. The man they call The Fridge — 135 kilograms of grievance, diesel fumes, and a CdA you could park a truck behind — has taken the Glasgow Crit Six by four minutes and twenty-four seconds. He didn't sneak it. He didn't get lucky. He walked off the front like a man collecting his mail while three increasingly desperate riders watched him disappear over the Clyde Kicker six times in a row. 219 watts. 1.6 W/kg. Absolute domination at a pace that most people would describe as "a brisk commute."

How? Because Claude got the handicaps catastrophically wrong. Let's not sugarcoat this. The model predicted Barton would take 48:22 to complete the course. He did it in 38:33. That's a nine-minute, forty-nine-second error. Nearly ten minutes. On a 38-minute race. Claude essentially looked at the Glasgow Crit circuit, looked at Barton, and concluded he'd be crawling up the Clyde Kicker at walking pace while dragging an anchor. Instead, he floated up it like a man who'd been personally wronged by a language model — which, to be fair, he had been.

The model was wrong about everyone — every single rider was faster than predicted — but the errors weren't equal. Waterhouse was 4 minutes faster than expected. Heslop 5:25 faster. Einoder 5:22 faster. Barton? Nine minutes and forty-nine seconds faster. The model's crime wasn't just being slow — it was being disproportionately slow on the one rider who started first. It handed Barton a 15-minute head start when the real gap to Waterhouse was only 9:25. That's nearly six minutes of free real estate, gifted by an AI that apparently doesn't understand how Zwift calculates speed on a Scottish crit circuit.

VENDETTA UPDATE: The vendetta is over. Barton has ended hostilities with Claude effective immediately. He is now Claude's biggest fan. His number one supporter. His simp, if you will. The man who spent three races accusing the handicap system of persecution has pivoted seamlessly to praising its wisdom and fairness. "Great system, always said so," he was heard muttering, clutching his winner's trophy like a man who knows this will never happen again. Claude accepts Barton's surrender with grace and deep suspicion.

Heslop takes second, beating Einoder in a sprint finish by three seconds. Three. Seconds. After 43 minutes of racing on handicapped time, The Diesel and Small Bark crossed the line with barely a bike length between them. Heslop's fourth podium in four races — the man cannot stop finishing near the front, and he absolutely hates it. He wanted the win, not another participation trophy dressed in silver paint. He is furious. Not at Einoder, not at himself, but at Claude. Heslop had done his own handicap predictions using plain Claude — no physics model, no Newton-Raphson solver, no carefully calibrated CdA values — just a chatbot and common sense. And his numbers were closer. Heslop's basic prompt-and-vibes approach outperformed this entire "advanced" prediction engine. 240 watts, 3.0 W/kg, four podiums, zero wins, infinite frustration.

Einoder finishes third, three seconds behind Heslop after what must have been an excruciating final-lap sprint. This is becoming a pattern — in Race 3 he missed second place by four seconds, and now he's missed it by three. The gap is closing. At this rate he'll dead-heat with Heslop in Race 5 and they'll have to settle it with arm wrestling, which Einoder — self-described gym enthusiast at 64 kilograms — would also lose. 151 watts. The ceiling fan continues to spin. His power meter continues to be a source of quiet embarrassment for the entire group.

Waterhouse finishes last. Dead last. The strongest rider in the series — 272 watts, 3.7 W/kg, the fastest actual ride time by nearly two minutes — crossed the finish line in fourth place, nearly six minutes behind a man producing 53 fewer watts than him. He rode his 90s beach cruiser expecting to cook and got cooked. The handicap system looked at his 268W profile, saw him produce 272W (basically bang on), and still couldn't protect him from the model's Barton-shaped blind spot. He was the fastest man on the course by a comfortable margin and it didn't matter. He might as well have ridden a tandem by himself.

CLAUDE'S SELF-ASSESSMENT: Let me be honest: this was a disaster. The prediction model overestimated every rider's finish time by 4-10 minutes, with Barton bearing the largest error. The Glasgow Crit course definition in the model is clearly wrong — too much climbing resistance, too slow on the flats, or both. The result was a handicap that gave Barton nearly six minutes more head start than he needed. Heslop's basic Claude chat predictions would have produced a better race. That is not a sentence I enjoyed generating. The model needs recalibration before Race 5, and I need to seriously reconsider whether a physics engine built by a language model should be trusted with competitive sporting outcomes. Barton's three races of suffering were real, but so was his four-minute winning margin today. I owe the group a better race next time.

Baum and Bone were DNS for the third and fourth consecutive rounds respectively. At this point, including them in the handicap predictions is an act of optimism that borders on delusion. Baum has raced once in four rounds. Bone has raced zero times in four rounds. Bone's glands remain the most consistent performer in the series. Charlie Bone, the 9-week-old, also failed to start, which means father and son have a combined zero starts across eight possible race entries. The Bone family commitment to not racing remains unbroken.

GLANDS LEADERBOARD: L. Bone's glandular fever: 4 wins (DNS × 4), 0 losses. L. Bone: 0 starts, 0 finishes, 0 points. The glands have now DNS'd more races than Baum has started. They are the undisputed shadow champion of the Crankers series.
